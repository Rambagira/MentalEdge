The Two-Minute Rule: Stop Procrastinating Now

If you look at your to-do list, it is likely filled with tiny tasks that you've been putting off for days. Replying to an email, washing a dish, or filing a document. These tasks accumulate and create "mental clutter."

David Allen, the author of Getting Things Done, introduced a simple rule to solve this: "If it takes less than two minutes, then do it now."

The Psychology of Open Loops

Why does this work? It's about cognitive load. When you tell yourself "I'll do that later," your brain opens a "file" for that task. This file stays open in your subconscious, draining your energy. This is known as an "Open Loop."

By executing the task immediately, you close the loop. You never have to think about it again. It takes more energy to remember to do a small task later than it does to simply do it now.

How to Implement It

  • Email Triage: When opening your inbox, if you can reply in 120 seconds, do not star it for later. Reply and archive.
  • Household Chores: Never put a dish in the sink. Put it in the dishwasher. It takes 30 seconds.
  • The Momentum Effect: Completing 3 or 4 tiny tasks in a row gives you a dopamine hit that propels you into larger, harder work.
Advanced Tip: This rule is also for starting habits. Don't commit to running 5 miles. Commit to "putting on your running shoes." That takes less than two minutes, but it usually leads to the run.
