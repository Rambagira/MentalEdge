How to Learn 2x Faster: The Feynman Technique

Most people study by re-reading the textbook or highlighting sentences. This is the "Illusion of Competence." You recognize the text, so you think you know it. But when the test comes, you go blank.

To truly learn, you must use Active Recall. The best way to do this is the Feynman Technique, named after the Nobel Prize-winning physicist Richard Feynman.

The 4-Step Method

  1. Choose a Concept: Write the name of the concept at the top of a blank sheet of paper.
  2. Teach it to a Child: Explain the concept in your own words as if you were teaching it to a 5-year-old. Do not use jargon. If you have to use complex words, you don't understand it yet.
  3. Identify Gaps: When you get stuck or your explanation gets messy, stop. That is your "knowledge gap." Go back to the source material to relearn that specific part.
  4. Simplify: Repeat the process until your explanation is simple and uses analogies.

Why It Works

Simplicity is the ultimate sophistication. When you force your brain to translate complex ideas into simple language, you build robust neural pathways. You move from "memorization" to "understanding."

Try Spaced Repetition:
Don't study the same thing for 5 hours in one day. Study it for 30 minutes a day for 10 days. The brain needs time to "forget" slightly so it has to work hard to retrieve the memory, which strengthens it.
